NYC Recycling Program History
In 1984 there was a garbage crisis in New York City. Many local and regional landfills were near capacity or already closed, and there was resistance to opening new ones. Mayor Koch proposed building five giant new incinerators in the city to handle the crisis. There was a public outcry over the plan and a battle ensued to stop it. Finally, in 1989 NYC passed Local Law 19, the NYC Recycling Law, which initiated the first mandatory recycling program in NYC. Thus began the program that has endured to this day, often with mixed success due to budget cuts, rule changes, and periodic suspensions.1 This visualization looks at the NYC recycling programâ€™s success rate in more recent times, from 2016 to 2019, by looking at capture rates of recyclable materials.

Definitions
Capture Rate: The percentage of a recyclable material that is put out for recycling. In other words, it's the weight of a particular material collected for recycling divided by the weight of all that material in the waste stream including its recycling component (e.g., the paper in your recycling bag divided by the paper in your trash bag and your recycling bag) expressed as a ratio (%). Weight is in tons.
Diversion Rate: total recycling / total waste
MGP: metal, glass, plastic, and beverage cartons

Takeaways

Trends: Small multiples show that months affect rates: Some boroughs dip down in July-Aug and others flatten over the summer months. MGP capture has shown a slow, steady increase over the period, while paper capture has remained flat.
Rankings (Four-Year Average, 2016–2019): For MGP capture, the most successful borough was Staten Island (78%), followed by Queens (70%), Manhattan (62%), Brooklyn (56%), and the Bronx (54%). For paper capture, the most successful borough was again Staten Island (45%), followed by Manhattan (41%), Brooklyn (40%), Queens (39%), and the Bronx (32%). The Bronx was the worst in both categories but has shown the most improvement in MGP capture over the 4 years (7%).
Impact: To measure the impact of these capture rates, one needs to take into account the population of the various boroughs. Although Staten Island has the best recycling capture, its population is by far the smallest, therefore the impact is not as great.
